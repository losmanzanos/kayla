What is EMDR?

Your brain already knows how to heal.
EMDR helps it finish the job.

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) is one of the most extensively researched trauma treatments in existence. It's endorsed by the World Health Organization, the American Psychological Association, and the Department of Veterans Affairs for PTSD treatment.

The basic principle: traumatic memories often get "stuck" in the brain's processing system in a way that keeps them feeling current and overwhelming, even years later. EMDR uses bilateral stimulation — typically guided eye movements, taps, or tones that alternate from left to right — to activate the brain's natural processing system and allow those stuck memories to be integrated. The result is that the memory doesn't disappear, but it loses its emotional charge. It becomes the past, not the present.

What EMDR Is Not

EMDR is not hypnosis. You're fully conscious and in control throughout the process. You don't have to describe your trauma in detail (though some discussion is involved). You won't be forced to go anywhere you're not ready to go. And it's not just for combat veterans — EMDR works for all kinds of trauma: childhood abuse, relational trauma, accidents, medical events, and the slower, smaller wounds that don't always get labeled as "trauma" but shaped you deeply anyway.

The 8 Phases of EMDR

EMDR has a structured protocol with eight phases — but it doesn't feel clinical. Phases 1 and 2 are about history-taking and building coping resources. You won't start processing memories until your system is ready. Phases 3–6 involve the actual reprocessing using bilateral stimulation. Phases 7 and 8 are closure and reassessment. The number of sessions varies — some people experience significant relief in 6–12 sessions; others need more time, especially with complex trauma.

EMDR + IFS: A Powerful Combination

When I combine IFS and EMDR, we do parts work first — building a relationship with the protective parts that might be reluctant about processing — and then use EMDR to process the memories those parts have been guarding. This combination tends to be gentler, more thorough, and more lasting than either approach alone.

Questions

What people ask about EMDR

Does EMDR really work?

Yes. EMDR is one of the best-studied trauma treatments we have. Multiple meta-analyses show it reduces PTSD symptoms significantly, often faster than other approaches. It's endorsed by the WHO, APA, and Department of Veterans Affairs. It doesn't work the same for everyone, but the evidence base is strong.

Will I have to talk about my trauma in detail?

No. EMDR doesn't require you to narrate everything that happened. You access the memory, but you don't have to describe it in detail. Many clients find this a significant relief — they can process trauma without having to re-tell it.

Can EMDR be done virtually?

Yes. I offer telehealth EMDR throughout Arizona. Virtual EMDR uses auditory bilateral stimulation (tones through headphones) or visual tracking through the screen, and has been shown to be equally effective as in-person for most clients.

I don't have a single traumatic event. Can EMDR still help?

Absolutely. Many of the most impactful things EMDR processes aren't single dramatic events — they're patterns of experience: the constant criticism from a parent, years of feeling unseen, relational wounds that accumulated over time. EMDR can process these just as effectively.

How many sessions will I need?

It depends on what you're processing. Simple, single-incident trauma can sometimes be significantly resolved in 6–12 sessions. Complex trauma from years of experience typically takes longer. We'll reassess regularly and you'll always know where we are.
