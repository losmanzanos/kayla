What is IFS?

The parts of you aren't problems to fix.
They're wisdom to understand.

Internal Family Systems (IFS) is a model of therapy that understands the mind as a system of distinct "parts" — each with its own perspective, feelings, history, and protective role. The inner critic. The perfectionist. The numb one. The people-pleaser. The one who shuts down when things get intense.

In IFS, none of those are character flaws. They're parts of you that developed in response to your life — and they've been doing their best. The goal isn't to get rid of them. It's to get curious about them, understand what they're protecting, and help them take on new, less exhausting roles.

The Core Idea: Self-Led Healing

IFS holds that underneath all the parts — underneath the anxiety and the numbness and the critic — there is a core Self. Not a perfect self. Not an absence of parts. But a center that is calm, curious, compassionate, and capable of leading the system. The work of IFS is about accessing that Self and allowing it to build relationships with your parts.

When parts feel understood by your Self rather than suppressed or managed, they naturally relax. The inner critic stops screaming. The perfectionist loosens its grip. The shutdown part starts to trust that it's safe to feel.

IFS for Trauma, Anxiety & Perfectionism

IFS is particularly powerful for trauma because it allows us to approach difficult experiences through the protective parts that formed in response to them — rather than going directly into the memory. This makes it gentler than many trauma approaches and less likely to overwhelm your system.

For anxiety and perfectionism, IFS helps us understand what those parts are protecting you from (usually something like: rejection, failure, being seen as not enough). When you understand the fear underneath, the behavior starts to make sense — and change becomes possible.

What IFS Sessions Look Like

An IFS session might look like a conversation about your week — noticing what parts showed up, what they wanted, how they interacted. It might involve slowing down and getting curious about a sensation or an emotion. It might involve speaking directly to a part of yourself and listening to what it needs. There are no scripts. We follow what's present for you.

Questions

About IFS therapy

Is IFS the same as "parts work"?

Yes. "Parts work" is the informal name for IFS-informed therapy. IFS is the specific model developed by Dr. Richard Schwartz, and it provides a structured, research-supported framework for working with the different parts of your internal experience.

Do I have to believe in the "parts" concept for IFS to work?

Not at all. You don't have to believe anything about it in advance. We just try it together and see what's useful. Most people find that the parts metaphor resonates in a way that purely intellectual talk therapy doesn't — because it matches how the mind actually feels from the inside.

Can IFS be combined with EMDR?

Yes, and I often combine them. IFS helps us identify and build a relationship with the parts that hold trauma before we do EMDR processing. This integration — sometimes called IFS-informed EMDR — tends to be gentler and more thorough than either approach alone.

How long does IFS therapy take?

It varies widely. Some clients experience significant shifts in a few months. Others work in IFS for years, going deeper as they build more trust with their system. There's no right timeline — just yours.
